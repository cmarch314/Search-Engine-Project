creators.</p>\n<blockquote><p><!--StartFragment -->\u00c2\u00a0As video sites look for ways to attract higher-quality content, they are dangling cash, usually offering to cut creators in on the advertising revenue their work generates.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Such models are likely to emerge in many User generated Content sites &#8212; it is only natural from business perspective.\u00c2\u00a0 What is also natural is that most people don&#8217;t want to remain in the long tail, they want to move as rapidly to the head as possible.\u00c2\u00a0 This is clear from exaples like:</p>\n<blockquote><p><!--StartFragment -->\u00c2\u00a0Kent Nichols, co-creator of Ask a Ninja, a series of comic videos in which a cranky ninja responds to viewer questions, says he managed to earn more than $20,000 last year on Revver. Mr. Nichols now has an agent, who recently helped him negotiate what he says is a more lucrative advertising deal with another company.</p></blockquote>\n<p>A very important thing is</p>\n<blockquote><p><!--StartFragment -->\u00c2\u00a0Just as Hollywood moguls have yet to find an infallible formula for producing a blockbuster, Internet video producers still don\u00e2\u20ac\u2122t know why some clips \u00e2\u20ac\u0153go viral,\u00e2\u20ac? sent by e-mail from person to person and incorporated into blog entries, and others languish, seen only by the
