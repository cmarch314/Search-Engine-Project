Atom, Bond and Conformer Indices</A>\n</H1>\n\n<P>\nOEChem assigns each OEAtomBase and each OEBondBase of an OEMolBase an\nindex when it is created.  This index can be used to distinguish one\nOEAtomBase from another, as that it is unique among OEAtomBases of\nthe same molecule.  Atom and bond indices are also stable. A given\nOEAtomBase will have the same index throughout its lifetime\nindependent of the reordering of atoms of a molecule, or the creation\nor deletion of other atoms (with the single exception of the <code>Sweep</code>\nmethod of OEMolBases and the <code>SweepConfs</code> method of OEMCMolBases).\n\n<P>\nNote that atom and bond indices are not guaranteed to be sequential,\nor even created sequentially, hence atom indices can't easily be used\nto retrieve all of the atoms of a molecule.  They can however be\nassumed to be dense small integers greater than or equal to zero and\nless than GetMaxAtomIdx() for atoms (or GetMaxBondIdx() for bonds)\n\n<P>\nOEAtomBases and OEBondBases both implement the GetIdx method to return\ntheir unique index in the molecule and also a SetIdx method for\nsetting a unique number. SetIdx should probably only be used in\nwriting file readers or other low-level methods. The following example\nshows loops over the atoms and bonds and prints their indices.\n\n<P>\n<dl><dd><pre class=\"verbatim\">#!/usr/bin/env python\n# ch9-1.py\nfrom openeye.oechem import *\n\nmol = OEGraphMol()\nOEParseSmiles(mol, \"c1ccccc1\")\n\nprint \"atoms\"\nfor atom in mol.GetAtoms():\n    print atom.GetIdx()\n\nprint \"bonds\"\nfor bond in mol.GetBonds():\n    print bond.GetIdx()\n</pre>\n<div class=\"verbatiminput-footer\">\n<a href=\"ch9-1.txt\" type=\"text/plain\">Download as text.</a>\n</div>\n</dd></dl>\n\n<P>\nEven typing the following idiom may invalidate any chance of support\nand eliminate any glimmer of respect from OpenEye Scientific Software\nor the computational chemistry/chemoinformatics community.\n\n<P>\n<dl><dd><pre class=\"verbatim\"># Never ever, ever do this!\n\nfor i in xrange(mol.NumAtoms()):\n    atom = mol.GetAtom(HasAtomIdx(i))\n    # pretend atom is valid\n</pre></dl>\n\n<P>\nThere are far more efficient methods of crashing computer software\nthat should be used instead.
