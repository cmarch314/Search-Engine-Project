<b>Prediction of chemical reactivity</b>:\n    <br/>\n    State of the art systems to predict chemical reactivity are either not high-throughput, \n    i.e., saddle-point methods with \n    QM approximations, or are brittle and manually encoded rule-based systems.\n    I work on a new machine learning approach to reaction prediction.  \n    This approach uses statistical ranking models learned \n    from databases of known synthetically productive reactions. \n    A web server predictor for a subset of chemistry can be found \n    <a href=\"http://reactions.igb.uci.edu/rxnpred/\">here</a>.\n  </li>\n  <li>\n    <b>Differential analysis of high-throughput data</b>:\n    <br/>\n    A common task in the analysis of data generated by high-throughput biological techniques \n    (such as DNA microarrays or Mass Spectrometry) is determining\n    which measurements are statistically different between two or more conditions.\n    This task is often undertaken in low replicate regimes.\n    I have helped to extend a Bayes-regularized approach to this problem. \n    A webserver implementing these methods can be found \n    <a href=\"http://cybert.ics.uci.edu\">here</a>.\n    I have used these methods (amongst others) in collaborations with several\n    immunology groups to analyze protein microarray data.\n
