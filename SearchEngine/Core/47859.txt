type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-experiential-computing\">\r\n\t\t<article>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\r\n\t\t\t<header class=\"entry-header\">\r\n    \t\t\t<h1 class=\"entry-title\">\r\n    \t\t\t\tEventWeb 13: Crowdsourcing    \t\t\t</h1><!-- .entry-title -->\r\n  \t\t\t</header>\r\n\r\n  \t\t\t\r\n  \t\t\t\r\n  \t\t\t<div class=\"entry-content clearfix\">\r\n    \t\t\t<p>Events are unique in the sense of attracting people.\u00c2\u00a0 People are attracted to attend events and know about past events.\u00c2\u00a0 More importantly, people are interested in <em><strong>reports</strong></em> on events.\u00c2\u00a0 And people really want to get reports on events from many different people in the hope that some of them will be similar to them in socio-economic-political orientations.\u00c2\u00a0 No wonder that when an important event takes place (such as a\u00c2\u00a0local team winning a championship) TV people interview many people asking silly questions like &#8212; how do you feel about our team winning championship.</p>\n<p>In this context, it is clear that media is slowly going towards crowdsourcing.\u00c2\u00a0 Wikipedia defines crowdsourcing as:</p>\n<blockquote><p><!--StartFragment --></p>\n<p>&#8220;<strong>Crowdsourcing</strong>&#8221; is a term coined by <a title=\"Wired magazine\" href=\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wired_magazine\"><em>Wired</em> magazine</a> writer Jeff Howe and editor Mark Robinson in June 2006. Like <a title=\"Outsourcing\" href=\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outsourcing\">outsourcing</a>, crowdsourcing is a model that depends on work being done outside the traditional company walls, but while outsourcing is typically performed by lower paid professionals, crowdsourcing relies on a combination of <a title=\"Volunteer\" href=\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volunteer\">volunteers</a> and low-paid amateurs who use their spare time to create content, solve problems, or even do corporate <a title=\"Research and development\" href=\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Research_and_development\">R&#038;D</a>.</p>\n<p>Crowds targeted for crowdsourcing include <a class=\"new\" title=\"Garage scientist\" href=\"http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Garage_scientist&#038;action=edit\">garage scientists</a>, amateur <a title=\"Videographer\" href=\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Videographer\">videographers</a>, <a title=\"Freelancer\" href=\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freelancer\">freelancers</a>, photo enthusiasts, data companies, writers, <a title=\"Smart mob\" href=\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smart_mob\">smart mobs</a> and the <a title=\"Electronic herd\" href=\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_herd\">electronic herd</a>.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Lately people have talked a lot about crowdsourcing News &#8212; commonly called citizen journalism.\u00c2\u00a0\u00c2\u00a0A Citizen journalist is a common person who reports &#8212; thru blog posts, photogaphs, videos &#8212; an event.\u00c2\u00a0 It is becoming increasingly common that when an unexpected event takes place, since there are no professional journalists to prepare a report (text, audio, or visual), most early reports are &#8216;published&#8217; by people who just happened to be there.\u00c2\u00a0 These people have their camera, also called mobile phone, and can capture the event using photographs, audio, and video.\u00c2\u00a0 Since there are many people with their camera, it is possible that many people capture the event and are willing to share their &#8216;report&#8217; with others.\u00c2\u00a0 No wonder that during the last bombing in London and Mumbai, as well as in some other situations like Katrina, most early and candid pictures came from such citizen journalists.\u00c2\u00a0 Even CNN used pictures and videos from citizen journalists in their reports.</p>\n<p>\u00c2\u00a0</p>\n<p>Let&#8217;s now consider a slightly different scenario.\u00c2\u00a0 Suppose that an event takes place.\u00c2\u00a0 And there is a <em><strong>place</strong></em> where citizen journalists (that is YOU) can send their reports (in any form &#8212; photos, video, audio, or text) and these reports somehow are attached to that event.\u00c2\u00a0 If people knew that this is possible, then they will start sending reports on all important events and not-so-important-events (like an accident in my neighbor hood or a wonderful ballett performance at the elementary school by my daughter) to this location.\u00c2\u00a0 Then you will have a\u00c2\u00a0<strong><em>place</em></strong> for people to publish their reports.\u00c2\u00a0 But more importantly, you will have a place to find reports on all events and from all sources.\u00c2\u00a0 Thus you will not be limited to a perspective only of a major TV station or a Newspaper, but of anybody, including those sources, making available a kaleidoscope of reports on the event of your interest.\u00c2\u00a0 Of course, there should be methods to organize these reports in some form so you can search only for particular types of perspectives.</p>\n<p>\u00c2\u00a0</p>\n<p>This scenario is not very different from what you see happening now a days in blogosphere or even on social networking oriented sites.\u00c2\u00a0 This is just a way to bring together power of new media to facilitate what people always wanted to do &#8212; to know about events and how different people react to events.</p>\n  \t\t\t</div>\r\n  \t\t\t<div
