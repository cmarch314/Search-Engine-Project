relations.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n<p>\nLet\n<i>a<sub>0</sub></i>(<i>r<sub>1</sub></i>)<i>a<sub>1</sub></i>,\n<i>a<sub>1</sub></i>(<i>r<sub>2</sub></i>)<i>a<sub>2</sub></i>,\n... ,\n<i>a<sub>(n-1)</sub></i>(<i>r<sub>n</sub></i>)<i>a<sub>n</sub></i>\nbe a chain of relations among intervals\n<i>a<sub>0</sub></i> through\n<i>a<sub>n</sub></i>.&nbsp;\nThen this chain of relations\nmay be used to infer that\n</p>\n\n<div class='eqn'>\n<i>a<sub>0</sub></i>(<i>r<sub>1</sub></i>.<i>r<sub>2</sub></i>.&nbsp;...&nbsp;.<i>r<sub>n</sub></i>)<i>a<sub>n</sub></i>\n</div>\n\n<p>For a collection of relations on intervals,\nwe can derive the <i>strongest implied relation</i>\nbetween\n<i>a<sub>0</sub></i> and <i>a<sub>n</sub></i>\nby examining all possible chains of inference between\n<i>a<sub>0</sub></i> and\n<i>a<sub>n</sub></i>,\nand taking the intersection of all\nthe resulting compositions of chained relations.&nbsp;\nEach chain of inference places a constraint on the relation,\nso the inferences are combined by taking their intersection.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n<p>\nThe number of possible chains rises very quickly\nas the number of relations in the collection is increased.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n<p>\nA related problem is determining,\nfor a particular collection of relations on indefinite intervals,\nwhether\nthere is any set of specific time values\nfor the intervals\nsuch that all the relations in the collection are true.&nbsp;\nThis is the <i>satisfaction</i> problem for Allen's interval algebra,\nand it has been shown to be NP-complete\n[<a href='#Vilain_Kautz_Beek1989-cpat'>Vilain+Kautz+Beek1989-cpat</a>].&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n<div class='figure'>\n<img src='img/Unsatisfiable.png' alt='Unsatisfiable graph'/>\n<p>\nFigure 1.&nbsp; Unsatisfiable relations\n</p>\n</div>\n\n<p>\nA simple example of a collection of relations and intervals\nthat is not satisfiable\nis three intervals <i>a</i>, <i>b</i>, and <i>c</i>\nsuch that\n<i>a</i><span class='ss'>(p)</span><i>b</i>,\n<i>b</i><span class='ss'>(p)</span><i>c</i>,\nand\n<i>c</i><span class='ss'>(p)</span><i>a</i>\n(each precedes the next, and the last precedes the first).&nbsp;\nThere are no definite intervals for which all these relations can hold.&nbsp;\nWe can calculate that this collection is unsatisfiable\nby inferring the relation that is implied between\n<i>a</i> and <i>c</i>\nby the other relations.&nbsp;\nThe inferred relation through <i>b</i>\nis\n</p>\n\n<div class='eqn'>\n(<i>a</i> to <i>b</i>).(<i>b</i> to <i>c</i>)\n=\n<span class='ss'>(p).(p)</span>\n=\n<span class='ss'>(p)</span>\n</div>\n\n<p>\nWe already know that the relation between\nas the relation between <i>c</i> and <i>a</i> is <span class='ss'>(P)</span>\n(the converse of the relation <span class='ss'>(p)</span>\nbetween <i>a</i> and <i>c</i>).&nbsp;\nThe strongest inferred relation between <i>c</i> and <i>a</i>\nis the intersection of these two relations\n</p>\n\n<div class='eqn'>\n<span class='ss'>(p)&cap; (P) = &empty;</span>\n</div>\n\n<p>\n&empty; means that <i>c</i> and <i>a</i> have no relation at all,\nwhich is not possible;\nso this collection of intervals and relations is unsatisfiable.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n\n<h2>Relationships between Allen relations</h2>\n\n<p>\nThe relationships between Allen relations\nare defined in terms of the corresponding sets of basic relations.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n<p>\nTwo Allen relations are equal\nif they contain the same basic relations,\nand not equal otherwise.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n<table frame='border' rules='all' class='FR' style='clear: right;'>\n<colgroup></colgroup>\n<thead>\n<tr><th colspan='2'>Weaker/stronger examples\n  </th>\n</tr>\n</thead>\n<tbody>\n<tr><td><span <i>stronger</i>).&nbsp;\nThis partial order is\nthe same as the partial order on the sets of basic Allen relations\ndefined by &sup;.&nbsp;\nRelation <i>A</i> is weaker than relation <i>B</i>\nif <i>A</i>&sup;<i>B</i>;\nthe &lt; and &sup; point in opposite directions.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n\n<h1>Eighteen maximal tractable subalgebras</h1>\n\n<table frame='border' rules='all' class='FR mtse' style='clear: right;'>\n<colgroup/>\n<thead>\n<tr><th colspan='2'><span class='mts'>A</span><sub>&equiv;</sub> examples\n  </th>\n</tr>\n</thead>\n<tbody>\n<tr><th>in</th><th>not in\n satisfaction in interval algebra is NP-complete,\nthere are subsets of the 8192 relations\nfor which satisfaction is tractable (a polynomial-time algorithm exists).&nbsp;\nOnce such subsets have been found,\na natural course of action is to\nmaximize their size by\nadding more relations,\nstopping just before an intractable subset results;\nthe result is a tractable subset\nthat cannot accept another relation without becoming intractable,\nand is thus maximal.&nbsp;\nIt turns out that there are eighteen maximal tractable subsets\nof Allen's interval algebra;\nevery tractable subset of the <i>algebras</i>,\nthat is, each is closed under\ncomposition, converse, intersection, and union\n(a set is <i>closed</i> under an operation if\nthe result of applying the operation to any element(s) of the set\nis another member of the set).&nbsp;\nThus they are usually described as\nmaximal tractable subalgebras.&nbsp;\n</p>\n<p>\nThe eighteen maximal tractable subalgebras are listed in Table 6,\nalong with rules defining which relations belong to them\nand links to the sets of elements in each one.&nbsp;\nThe most important one is the <span class='cu'>H</span> or Horn subalgebra;\nit is the only one of the eighteen that contains all 13 of the basic relations,\nand\ninference in it can be done using the path consistency algorithm\nrather than a more complex one.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n<table class='ss'>(pmMP).(mo) = (pseSdfOMP)</span>.&nbsp;\nInference of missing edges is equivalent\nto solving for satisfaction,\nwhich in the general case is NP-complete.&nbsp;\nFor the <span class='mts'>H</span> subclass,\nthe path-consistency algorithm may be used\nfor complete inference and determining satisfiability\n[<a href='#Gennari1998-trcp'>Gennari1998-trcp</a> p.194ff].&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n\n<h2>Equivalence of two networks</h2>\n\n<p>\nTwo complete networks are equivalent if\nthey contain the same intervals,\nand the relations between corresponding intervals are the same.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n<p>\nIf one or both the networks are not complete,\nthen they are equivalent if\nthe corresponding completed networks are equivalent.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n\n<h2>Specialization of a network</h2>\n\n<p>\nA complete network &alpha; is a <i>specialization</i>\nof another complete network &beta;\nif\n</p>\n\n<ol>\n  <li> <i>Nodes</i>(&alpha;) &supe; <i>Nodes</i>(&beta;)<br/>\n    (every interval in &beta; is present in &alpha;)\n  </li>\n  <li> For all corresponding edges <i>a</i> of &alpha;\n    and <i>b</i> of &beta;,\n    <i>a</i>&ge;<i>b</i> <br/>\n    (every relation in &alpha; is the same or stronger\n    than the corresponding relation in &beta;)\n  </li>\n</ol>\n\n<p>\nIf one or both the networks are not complete,\nthen\n&alpha; specializes &beta;\nif the completion of &alpha; specialized the completion of &beta;.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n<p>\nInformally,\na network can be specialized into\nanother network that meets all the first one's constraints.&nbsp;\nThis can't happen if the specialization doesn't have all the intervals,\nnor if the specialization has a weaker edge.&nbsp;\nA mnemonic is \"specialization means bigger and stronger\".&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n\n<div class='figure'>\n<img src='img/AllenBasicRelationsPO-vertical.png'\nalt='Partial order of the basic relations'/>\n<p>Figure 4.&nbsp; Partial order of the basic relations\n</p>\n</div>\n\n<h1>Ordering the basic relations</h1>\n\n<p>\nThe standard order I selected isn't the only plausible one.&nbsp;\nFigure 4 shows a partial order of the basic relations,\nin which each relation is obtained from the one(s) immediately above it\nby nudging one end of <i>a</i> to or beyond the next end of <i>b</i>.&nbsp;\n</p>\n\n\n<h1>Other names and symbols for the basic
