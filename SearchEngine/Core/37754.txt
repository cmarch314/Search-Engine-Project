Hence the fundamental ``object'' or data structure within a\nchemical information system is that of the molecule, its atoms and its\nbonds.\n\n<P>\nA significant problem encountered in such systems is that different\napplications place differing requirements or constraints on how a\nmolecule is represented. In protein biochemistry, molecules are divided\ninto amino acid residues with specific atom naming and conformational\ninformation such as alpha helix or beta sheet. In inorganic chemistry\nrequires isotopic and co-ordination information on each atom which\ncomplex chiralities. One possible solution is to prescribe a single data\nstructure that encodes all of the potential information required of an\natom. However, such an approach suffers from the fact that ``you can't\nplease all of the chemists, all of the time.'' A requirement in the field\nof chemical databases and substructure searching is that a molecule\nrepresentation be as compact as possible, to allow as much information\nto be held in memory as possible and maximize the performance of\nprocessing databases from disk.\n\n<P>\nThe alternative, even complementary, approach taken by OEChem is to\nconcentrate on the similarities in molecule processing, rather than on\nthe differences.
