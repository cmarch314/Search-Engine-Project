Synaptic long term potentiation (LTP) </b></font><font color=\"#999999\">\n<P>\n<b><font color=\"#996666\">Long-term potentiation (LTP).  </b><font color=\"#999999\">  Throughout these models is the phenomenon of synaptic LTP, by which synapses can be permanently changed in response to brief input.  LTP is a biological mechanism that is widely thought by neuroscientists to underlie human long-term memory.  It is currently the only such candidate mechanism, since it is the only biological mechanism known that satisfies three primary constraints imposed by the phenomenon of long-term memory: <br>\ni) it can be induced by even very brief activation (seconds), just as we are capable of learning a new item (e.g., a face, or a name) with just brief exposure; <br>\nii) it can last as long as memories do, e.g., potentially for decades, even after only brief exposure; and <br>\niii) it has enough capacity to store the memories of a lifetime, which may number in the tens or hundreds of millions of individual items.<br>\nThese three constraints of rapid induction, long duration, and high capacity determine the characteristics that any candidate mechanism for long-term memory must have.  These characteristics alone give rise to surprising computational results, and intriguing predictions.  <i><font color=\"#9999ff\">[Coultrip, R. and Granger, R. (1994). Non-Hebbian properties of LTP enable high-capacity encoding of temporal sequences.   Proc. Nat'l. Acad. Sci.,  91: 10104-10108]</i><font color=\"#999999\">.\n<P>\n<b><font color=\"#996666\">LTP occurs only at glutamate receptors.</b><font color=\"#999999\">  LTP occurs uniquely on particular types of synapses in the brain: those containing receptors for the neurotransmitter glutamate.  Other neurotransmitters are more famous than glutamate: Serotonin is the neurotransmitter targeted by the drugs Prozac, Paxil, and Zoloft; the GABA neurotransmitter is affected by Valium; and most Alzheimer's drugs affect the neurotransmitter Acetylcholine. Since many important diseases consist in part of damage to cells containing glutamate receptors, including Alzheimer's disease, schizophrenia, attention-deficit disorder, and autism, the pharmacological enhacement of the remaining glutamate receptors may have an ameliorative effect in some or all of these conditions.
