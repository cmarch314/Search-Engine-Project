Since the underlying storage\nmechanism is not exposed, there is no longer the notion of an array of\natoms or and array of bonds inside a molecule. In order to traverse\nthe atoms or bonds in C++, one uses iterators, but in Python we have\nwrapped the C++ iterators into generator methods much like the loop\nover molecules in a oemolistream or tag,value pairs of SD data.\n\n<P>\nThe two methods are GetAtoms() and GetBonds(). For each\nsuccessive iteration through the <code>for</code> loop, these methods return the\nnext atom and bond respectively.\n\n<P>\nThe following example show how to traverse the atoms and print out\ntheir atomic numbers and then traverse the bonds and print their bond\norder.\n\n<P>\n<dl><dd><pre class=\"verbatim\">#!/usr/bin/env python\n# ch6-1.py\nfrom openeye.oechem import *\n\nmol = OEGraphMol()\nOEParseSmiles(mol, \"c1ccccc1\")\n\nprint \"atoms\"\nfor atom in mol.GetAtoms():\n    print atom.GetAtomicNum()\nprint \"bonds\"\nfor bond in mol.GetBonds():\n    print bond.GetOrder()\n</pre>\n<div class=\"verbatiminput-footer\">\n<a href=\"ch6-1.txt\" type=\"text/plain\">Download as text.</a>\n</div>\n</dd></dl>\n\n<P>\nNote that this example also introduces a couple of new methods, an\nOEAtomBase member function: GetAtomicNum() and an OEBondBase member\nfunction: GetOrder().
