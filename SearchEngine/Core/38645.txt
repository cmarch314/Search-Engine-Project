Biopolymer Residues</A>\n<br><hr>\n</DIV>\n<!--End of Navigation Panel-->\n\n<H1><A NAME=\"SECTION0029200000000000000000\">\n26.2 Extensions to Daylight SMILES</A>\n</H1>\n\n<P>\nThe OEChem SMILES parsers support several minor extensions to Daylight\nsyntax.  Each of these extensions and its motivations are listed\nbelow.\n\n<P>\n<DL>\n<DT><STRONG>Quadruple Bond</STRONG></DT>\n<DD>In addition to ``-'', ``='' and ``#'' for specifying single, double and\ntriple bonds respectively, OEChem also supports ``$'' for specifying\nquadruple bonds.  An example would be octachlorodirhenate (III), which\nis written as ``<code>[Rh-](Cl)(Cl)(Cl)(Cl)$[Rh-](Cl)(Cl)(Cl)Cl</code>''.\n\n<P>\n</DD>\n<DT><STRONG>Unquoted and Additional Elements</STRONG></DT>\n<DD>In addition to the standard Daylight unquoted elements, B, C, N, O, F,\nP, S, Cl, Br and I, OEChem's SMILES readers also allow H, D and T to\nspecify hydrogen, deuterium and tritium.  Additionally, to support\nSyracuse SMILES, ``CL'' and ``BR'' are considered ``Cl'' and ``Br''.  The\nperiodic table is also extended from 102 to 109 elements, <I>i.e.</I>\n<code>[Sg]</code> for Seaborgium, with the addition of <code>[D]</code> and\n<code>[T]</code> representing <code>[2H]</code> and <code>[3H]</code> respectively.\n\n<P>\nOEChem may support ``<code>Na</code>'', ``<code>Li</code>'' and ``<code>K</code>'' as\nunquoted elements to support Syracuse SMILES at some point in the future.\n\n<P>\n</DD>\n<DT><STRONG>Aromatic Tellurium</STRONG></DT>\n<DD>In order to support OpenEye's aromaticity model, which allows\nTellurium to be aromatic, the SMILES parser has been extended to\nsupport ``<code>[te]</code>'', such as in tellurophene, ``<code>[te]1cccc1</code>'',\nwhich follows in the sequence furan (``<code>o1cccc1</code>''),\nthiophene (``<code>s1cccc1</code>'') and selenophene (``<code>[se]1cccc1</code>'').\n\n<P>\n</DD>\n<DT><STRONG>Atom Maps in Molecules</STRONG></DT>\n<DD>Traditionally, SMILES atom maps, <I>i.e.</I> <code>[Pb:1]</code>, are only ever\nused and\nspecified in reaction molecules, <code>[Pb:1]&#187;[Au:1]</code>.  However, OEChem\nextends this notion to allow atom maps to be used in discrete molecules.\nThis is often useful for denoting significant sites or attachment points\nin a molecule.  Traditionally in SMILES, isotopes of element zero have\nbeen used to perform role, however in OEChem both <code>[*:1]</code> and\n<code>[1*]</code> may be used.\n\n<P>\n</DD>\n<DT><STRONG>RGroup Attachment Points</STRONG></DT>\n<DD>As a short hand to support specifying templates for combinatorial\nlibraries, and to support existing Cactus and JChem/Marvin usage,\nOEChem allows ``[R2]'' to be used as short-hand for [*:2].  For\ninquiring minds, the SMILES [R2:3] is interpreted as [*:3] or [R3],\nwith the last specification taking priority.\n\n<P>\n</DD>\n<DT><STRONG>External Bond Attachment Points</STRONG></DT>\n<DD>OEChem SMILES also allows supports external closures, or potentially\nunsatisfied \"ring closures\".  These have the syntax, ampersand followed\nby a ring closure specification, <I>i.e.</I> an optional bond order followed\nby either a digit or a `%' character and two digits.  The index space\nof external bonds and ring closures is separate, so that the ring\nclosure ``2'' and the external attachment point ``&amp;2'' are unrelated.\n\n<P>\nWhen external attachment points are paired within a SMILES string,\nthey behave identically to ring closures, just using a separate index\nspace.  Hence, the SMILES ``c&amp;1ccccc&amp;1'' is interpreted the same\nway as ``c1ccccc1'', and ``C&amp;1.C&amp;1'' is interpreted like ``C1.C1'',\ni.e the SMILES ``CC''.\n\n<P>\nHowever, unlike ring closures, unpaired external attachment points\nare allowed and are interpreted like RGroup attachment points above.\nHence, the SMILES ``<code>CC&amp;1</code>'' (on its own) is equivalent to the RGroup\nattachment SMILES <code>CC[R1]</code>, which is equivalent to the atom mapped\nmolecule <code>CC[*:1]</code>.\n\n<P>\nThe major advantage of these semantics, inspired by Daylight's CHUCKLES,\nis that it allows convenient enumeration of combinatorial libraries\nusing string concatenation.  For example, three components of a library\nmay be specified as ``<code>C&amp;1CCC&amp;2</code>'', ``<code>F&amp;1</code>'' and\n``<code>Br&amp;2</code>''.  The using the same notation\n``<code>C&amp;1CCC&amp;2.F&amp;1.Br&amp;2</code>'' is interpreted as the reaction\nproduct, <I>i.e.</I> ``<code>FCCCCBr</code>''.\n\n<P>\nAs with ring closures, bond orders may be specified after the ampersand\nand before the closure index, ``<code>C&amp;=1</code>'', and two digit closures are\nindicated by a '<code>%</code>' prefix, <I>i.e.</I> ``<code>C&amp;%12</code>'' or\n``<code>C&amp;=%12</code>''.\n\n<P>\n</DD>\n</DL>\n\n<P>\n\n<DIV CLASS=\"navigation\">\n<p><hr>\n<table align=\"center\" border=\"0\" width=\"100%\" cellpadding=\"0\" cellspacing=\"0\">\n<tr>\n<td rowspan=2><A HREF=\"node216.html\"><img src=\"../icons/previous.gif\"\n  border=\"0\" alt=\"Previous Page\"></A></td>\n<td rowspan=2><A HREF=\"node215.html\"><img src=\"../icons/up.gif\"\n  border=\"0\" alt=\"Up One Level\"></A></td>\n<td rowspan=2><A HREF=\"node218.html\"><img src=\"../icons/next.gif\"\n  border=\"0\" alt=\"Next Page\"></A></td><td width=\"100%\" rowspan=\"2\"> </td>\n<td valign=\"center\" align=\"right\" height=\"56\" background=\"../icons/title_bkgd.gif\" colspan=4 class=\"navigation\">OEChem - C++ Theory Manual\n<BR>\nVersion 1.3.1</td></tr>\n<tr><td><img src=\"../icons/spacer_1.gif\"\n  border=\"0\" alt=\"\"></td>\n<td><A href=\"contents.html\"><img src=\"../icons/contents.gif\"\n  border=\"0\" alt=\"Contents\"></A></td>\n<td><img src=\"../icons/spacer_2.gif\"\n  border=\"0\" alt=\"\"></td>\n<td><img src=\"../icons/blank_4.gif\"\n  border=\"0\" alt=\"\"></td>\n</tr></table>\n<b class=\"navlabel\">Previous:</b> <a class=\"sectref\" HREF=\"node216.html\">26.1 Daylight SMILES</A>\n<b class=\"navlabel\">Up:</b> <a class=\"sectref\" HREF=\"node215.html\">26.
