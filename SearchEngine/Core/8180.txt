0pt;\">We develop an\r\nalgorithm for finding and kinematically tracking multiple people in\r\nlong sequences. Our basic assumption is that people tend to take on\r\ncertain canonical poses, even when performing unusual activities like\r\nthrowing a baseball or figure skating. We create a <b>fully automatic</b> system that works in three stages. First, it detects people in lateral walking poses. The system then builds a discriminitive appearance model for each limb from the walking detections; we assume the features that discriminate a person in one frame will discriminate the person in other frames. Finally, the system uses the limb models in a pictorial structure framework, detecting figures in unrestricted poses in both previous and successive frames. We have run our tracker on hundreds of thousands of frames, and present and apply a methodology for evaluating\r\ntracking on such a large scale. We test our tracker on real sequences\r\nincluding a feature-length film, an hour of footage from a public park,\r\nand various sports sequences. We find that we can quite accurately\r\nautomatically find and track multiple people interacting with each\r\nother while performing fast and unusual motions. </p>\r\n      <br>\r\n      <br>\r\n      </font><!-- ***Paper Citation//-->\r\n      <p> Ramanan, D., Forsyth, D.
